## The SaaS trap

There's a pattern so ubiquitous in enterprise software that most people don't even question it anymore: the longer you use a SaaS product, the more it costs. Seat-based pricing creeps up. Feature tiers multiply. Bundles get restructured to capture more spend. The vendor's costs go down as they scale; your costs go up.

This is not a law of nature. It's a business model choice.

## The cost collapse nobody's talking about

Software development is no longer expensive. The work that used to require large engineering teams — building features, maintaining integrations, shipping updates — is rapidly being automated. AI agents can write code, run tests, and deploy changes at a fraction of the historical cost.

But instead of passing those savings to customers, most SaaS vendors are doing the opposite. They're using AI to improve margins while keeping prices steady or raising them. The cost collapse is real, but the benefits are being captured by the platform, not the users.

## The reversal

Haderach takes the opposite approach. The platform runs at cost-plus — operating expenses passed through with a transparent markup, no hidden fees, no feature gates.

The more users join, the cheaper it gets for everyone. That's not a marketing claim — it's arithmetic. Shared infrastructure, amortized AI costs, and community-contributed process improvements all create economies of scale that flow back to customers.

No plans. No bundles. No seat limits. No features held hostage behind paywalls. Think about what you want, use what's there, build what's missing — then pay cost-plus for what you actually use.

## Why this matters for the market

The anti-SaaS reversal isn't just a pricing strategy — it's a trust signal. In a world where enterprise buyers are increasingly skeptical of vendor lock-in and opaque pricing, cost transparency becomes a competitive advantage.

When your vendor's incentives are aligned with yours — when they benefit from efficiency, not from complexity — the relationship changes fundamentally. You stop being a revenue target and start being a partner.

## Industry collaboration

There's a second-order effect that most people miss: when the platform's economics encourage broad adoption, it becomes possible for competitors to collaborate on shared non-competitive processes. Payroll, vendor management, compliance reporting — these aren't differentiators. They're table stakes that every company builds independently at enormous cost.

An agent-facilitated platform with cost-plus economics makes industry-wide collaboration on these shared problems not just possible, but economically rational. The whole industry gets better, not just one company.
