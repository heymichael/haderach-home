## The gap between knowing and building

Every company has the same problem: the people who understand the business best are not the people who build the software. Domain experts know what needs to change, but they can't change it themselves. Instead, they write requirements documents, sit through sprint planning meetings, and wait weeks for an engineer to translate their intent into code.

This gap is expensive, slow, and lossy. Something always gets lost in translation.

## Nobody has to touch code

Haderach eliminates this gap by putting an AI agent between the practitioner and the platform. The practitioner describes what they need in plain conversation. The agent translates that into product changes — data model updates, new workflows, interface adjustments — without the practitioner ever touching code.

This isn't a chatbot answering questions. It's a product manager that listens, asks clarifying questions, proposes solutions, and implements changes through a structured tool interface. The practitioner's domain expertise steers what gets built; the agent handles the how.

## Strategic conversation as the interface

The shift is subtle but profound: the primary interface for shaping the product is no longer a feature request form or a JIRA ticket. It's a strategic conversation.

When a practitioner says "we need to track vendor spend by department and flag anything over 10% of budget," they're not filing a ticket — they're participating in product development. The agent can respond with a proposal, show what the implementation would look like, and execute it once approved.

This turns every user into a potential product contributor, without requiring any technical skills.

## What this means for organizations

In traditional software, the feedback loop is long: user frustration → support ticket → product backlog → sprint prioritization → development → testing → release → maybe what the user wanted. Each step introduces delay and distortion.

In an agent-native platform, the loop collapses: practitioner intent → agent conversation → proposed change → approval → live. Days become minutes.

The organizational implications are significant. Senior managers spend less time translating between business needs and engineering capacity. Frontline users feel heard and empowered instead of frustrated and ignored. And the platform evolves at the speed of the business, not the speed of the development team.

## In the age of AI, practitioners are more essential than ever

There's a common fear that AI will replace knowledge workers. The opposite is true in this model. AI handles the implementation — the part that was always a bottleneck. But the strategic thinking, the domain expertise, the judgment about what matters and what doesn't — that becomes more valuable, not less.

Practitioners aren't being replaced. They're being promoted from powerless requestors to empowered shapers of the product they use every day.
